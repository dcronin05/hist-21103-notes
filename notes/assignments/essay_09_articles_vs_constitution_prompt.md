## Professor's Guidance Notes

Below is a structured summary of the key themes explained by **Professor Jim Ross** in his video lecture (see the **[Auto-Generated Video Transcript](./articles_vs_constitution_video_notes.md)**).

### 1. Who Holds Power? (State Sovereignty vs. Popular Sovereignty)
* **The Articles of Confederation (Power in the States):** 
  * Article II states: *"Each state retains its sovereignty, freedom, and independence."* 
  * Sovereignty means absolute power. Under the Articles, the individual states held the ultimate power, and the national government was extremely weak.
* **The U.S. Constitution (Power in the People):**
  * The Preamble opens with: *"We, the People of the United States... do ordain and establish this Constitution."*
  * The authority to create and run the government comes from the people (popular sovereignty), rather than from the states.
  * **Historical Context on "The People":** Originally, this meant white property-owning men. The definition of "the people" expanded over time through historical struggle (non-property owners in the 1830s, African Americans in the 1870s, women in the 1920s).

### 2. Organization of Government (Unicameral vs. Co-Equal Branches)
* **Under the Articles:**
  * Only **one branch** of government existed: a unicameral legislature (Congress).
  * There was no President (executive) and no independent national court system (judiciary).
  * This weak structure was chosen because colonists feared creating a new central tyranny like the British monarchy they had just fought.
* **Under the Constitution:**
  * Created **three co-equal branches** of government with a system of checks and balances:
    1. **Legislative Branch** (Article I) — Congress (Senate and House of Representatives).
    2. **Executive Branch** (Article II) — The President.
    3. **Judicial Branch** (Article III) — The Supreme Court and lower federal courts.
  * No single branch is above the others; they are co-equal and hold distinct duties.

### 3. What is Missing? (The Bill of Rights)
* **A Critical Omission:** A major element is completely missing from both the Articles of Confederation and the original 1787 U.S. Constitution: **The Bill of Rights** (the first ten amendments protecting individual liberties).
* **The Hint:** Dr. Ross points out that the Bill of Rights is at the very end of the modern Constitution but was not part of the original 1787 draft. It was added later (ratified in 1791) to satisfy Anti-Federalist concerns about federal overreach.
