## Professor's Guidance Notes

Below is a structured summary of the key themes explained by **Professor Jim Ross** in his video lecture (see the **[Auto-Generated Video Transcript](./commonwealth_video_notes.md)**).

### 1. Defining the Commonwealth
* **The Core Definition:** A commonwealth represents the ideal balance between private enterprise (commercial interest) and the common good of all citizens.
* **Dual Purpose:** Virginia functioned both as a private commercial venture (to generate profit for London investors) and as a public society designed to address social issues in England (absorbing the poor, landless, and prisoners).

### 2. The Three Intellectual Pillars of the Commonwealth
According to Horn and Dr. Ross, the Commonwealth idea synthesized three major sources:
1. **Changing Circumstances:** The practical and material developments within the colony that forced changes in policy.
2. **The Church's Teachings (St. Augustine's Influence):**
   * Heavily shaped by 4th-century North African theologian St. Augustine's *The City of God*.
   * Augustine argued that God is sovereign over all and that human government is a divine gift designed to promote the common good and restrain human evil. This view was strongly embraced by English Puritans.
3. **Civic Humanism:**
   * A secular classical theory that government power originates from "We the People" agreeing to organize together.
   * *Note on U.S. Foundations:* Dr. Ross highlights that the U.S. Constitution and Declaration of Independence represent a synthesis of these two philosophies—e.g., "All men are created equal... by their Creator" (Augustinian theology) paired with "We the People" (Civic Humanism).

### 3. Core Principles & Institutions
* **Hierarchy and Good Order:** The 16th/17th-century commonwealth model relied on hierarchy. Commentators believed that since human nature is innately selfish, the happiness of the people rested on the virtue and quality of the governor's judgment.
* **Balanced Government:** Power was shared between a Monarch (representing unity) and a Parliament (speaking for the people).
* **Consent of the Governed:** Any new tax or law required the consent of the people (initially defined as landowning males). As the colony grew, this concept of consent was institutionalized locally through representative bodies (the House of Burgesses/Assembly).
* **The Church as a Social Anchor:** The Anglican Church was the institutional anchor of the commonwealth, holding society together and providing a collective moral vocabulary for human relationships.
* **The Colony's Progress (1619–1621):** By 1621, 3,500 settlers had arrived in 42 ships. English laws and representative government were established, diverse crop cultivation and manufactures were expanding along the James River, and plans were made for a free school (p. 152). This expansion, however, created direct conflict with local Native Americans.
