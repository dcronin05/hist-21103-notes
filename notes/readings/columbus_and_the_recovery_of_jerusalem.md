# Columbus and the Recovery of Jerusalem

**Author:** Abbas Hamdani  
**Affiliation:** University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee  
**Source:** *Journal of the American Oriental Society*, Vol. 101, No. 1 (Jan. - Mar., 1981), pp. 39-48  
**Stable URL:** [JSTOR](https://www.jstor.org/stable/598947)

---

> **Abstract / Overview**  
> The beginning of Columbus' voyages to the New World coincides with the culmination of the Spanish Reconquista and carries over the latter's crusading zeal. Columbus' Diaries reveal that his *empresa de las Indias* had involved a master plan of contacting the Mongol and pro-Christian Grand Khan in the East, circumventing and encircling the Islamic lands of the Middle East, opening a new trade route to the East that did not have to pass through Mamluk territories and combining the forces of Western and Eastern Christendom in an enveloping movement for the recovery of Jerusalem and its *casa santa*.[^1] Columbus' plan followed a long tradition of Marco Polo (1295), Marino Sanudo's *Liber Secretorum* (1321), the life-work of Pope Pius II (1458–1465) and such writings as Campanus' *Oratio* (1471).

---

## Introduction: The Crusading Milieu of 1492

THE YEAR 1492 IS AN IMPORTANT MILESTONE in the histories of Europe, Islam and America. It marks the culmination of the process of Reconquista with the capture of Granada (January 2) which ended the Muslim political presence in Spain. It also marks the beginning of a new era in Columbus' discovery of America (October 12). This discovery gave such an impetus to exploration and expansion, colonization and nationalism, that it is looked upon as a great event of the Renaissance and as heralding the modern age. And justifiably so.

Yet its birth in a medieval crusading milieu is most often underrated, if not totally forgotten. It is this medieval background and the medieval crusading spirit that I want to describe, as the principal motivating factor responsible for the discovery of the New World. I will not go into the controversy surrounding the question as to who discovered America first.[^2] My concern will only be with Columbus and his interest in the recovery of Jerusalem.

---

## Historical Clarifications

First, however, it would appear necessary to make two brief clarifications:

### 1. Historicity of Columbus's Sources

First, regarding the historicity of Columbus' discovery. In this endeavor two studies stand out as most valuable, namely: Samuel Morison's *Admiral of the Ocean Sea*[^3] and Antonio Ballesteros' *Cristóbal Colón*.[^4] Both authors have relied on the best sources available, such as the *Journals of Columbus*[^5] and the writings of contemporaries and near contemporaries such as Las Casas,[^6] Peter Martyr,[^7] Fernández de Oviedo[^8] and Columbus' son Ferdinand.[^9]

They have successfully defended the historicity of these sources against what Morison calls the "debunker" school of Vignaud[^10] that asserts that Columbus was only looking for a mythical island called Antillas in the Atlantic, that he was wholly guided by a mysterious pilot and that his proposal to go to Asia and other details of his journals are all fabrications. Such a contrary opinion need not detain us here as this controversy seems, by now, well settled in favor of the historicity of Columbus' discovery.

### 2. Meaning of the "Indian Enterprise"

The second clarification concerns the description that Columbus gives to his voyages time and again. He calls them his *empresa de las Indias*, "the Indian enterprise."

> "The word India in the Middle Ages," says Charles Nowell, "had no exact geographical meaning to Europeans; it was a convenient expression denoting the East beyond the Mohammedan world."[^11]

Thus Columbus' enterprise was to reach the Orient or Asia or India, not eastward via the Muslim lands, but by circumventing them and going westward via the "Ocean Sea."

---

## The Motivating Factor: Recovery of Jerusalem

Columbus could not have undertaken such an ambitious enterprise without strong motivation. In a brilliant study such as Morison's one would expect to read about that motivation. Morison, however, describes his objective otherwise:

> "My main concern is with the Columbus of action, the Discoverer.... I am content to leave his 'psychology', his 'motivation' and all that to others."

Nonetheless, he gives us an important clue when he writes:

> "Christopher Columbus belonged to an age that was past, yet he became the sign and symbol of this new age of hope, glory and accomplishment. His medieval faith impelled him to a modern solution: expansion. If the Turk could not be pried loose from the Holy Sepulchre, by ordinary means, let Europe seek new means overseas; and he, Christopher the Christ-bearer, would be the humble yet proud instrument of Europe's regeneration."[^12]

Here Morison states the motivation correctly, but shies away from further analysis, concerning himself instead with navigational and technical problems of Columbus' voyages. Ballesteros, for his part, has a better feel for the crusading environment which obtained in 15th century Spain, yet, like Morison, he, too, concerns himself more with Columbus the man and explorer than with Columbus the crusader.

Washington Irving was the first to draw attention to Columbus' crusading aim for the conquest of Jerusalem. He describes Columbus' objectives as:

> "the discovery of the New World, the conversion of the Gentiles and the recovery of the Holy Sepulchre: three events which he supposed to be pre-destined to succeed each other."[^13]

Having stated this, Irving does not explore the connection between the westward voyages of Columbus and the recovery of the Holy Sepulchre.

In recent years, John Phelan in his *Millennial Kingdom of the Franciscans* has made an excellent study of the Franciscan connections of Columbus and the evolution of Columbus' mind. Phelan thinks that in 1492 and 1498 Columbus was motivated by the conventional crusading ideal. In 1501–02, however, Columbus linked the crusading tradition to an apocalyptical vision of himself as a Messiah.[^14]

This view would accord with the general argument of this paper if Phelan's exposé does not indicate only a symbolic or allegorical conquest of Jerusalem; for to my mind, Columbus was too much a man of action, even in his last years, to be satisfied with an imaginary conquest. His real desire was for the actual deliverance of Jerusalem from Muslim hands, and to open a new route for doing so.

---

## Three Approaches to the Conquest of Jerusalem

This could be done, according to Columbus, in three ways:

- **Approach A**: By contacting the pro-Christian Mongol Grand Khan in the East to which he was supposedly going via the westward ocean-route. This would bring Western and Eastern Christianity into a common struggle to recover Jerusalem from Muslim rule.
- **Approach B**: By using the resources of the newly discovered lands to conquer Jerusalem within three years.
- **Approach C**: By the force of the messianic personality of Columbus himself as prophesied by St. Jerome and Abbot Joachim.

### A. Contacting the Grand Khan & Mongol Alliance

Such an enterprise implied circumventing and then crushing the then central Muslim power, the Mamluks of Egypt and Syria, who were the guardians of the Muslim holy places, Mecca and Medina, and to whom most of the Muslim world looked for leadership. The Mamluks were responsible for taking Jerusalem finally in 1244 (while yet in the service of the last Ayyubid), Antioch in 1268 and Acre in 1291, thus obliging the gradual evacuation of the crusading armies from the Eastern Mediterranean. They were also responsible in 1260 for permanently halting the westward push of the pro-Christian Mongols, at 'Ayn Jalut in Palestine, thereby frustrating the great entente eagerly sought between the Pope and the Princes of Christendom, on the one hand, and the Grand Khan of Karakorum and Peking and the Il-Khan of Tabriz and Baghdad, on the other, for the common purpose of recapturing Jerusalem.

There was another dimension to this grand design—trade. The Muslim regime of the Mamluks lay astride the international East-West trade route by virtue of their holding the areas bordering on the Red Sea. The Mamluk Sultan Ashraf Barsbay (1422–1438) had captured Cyprus in 1424–26, thus bringing to an end a long-standing crusading kingdom. He instituted a monopoly of the sugar-trade and imposed such transit dues and currency restrictions on the European trade with the Orient that protests began to be expressed by the Italian maritime city-states.[^15]

Another cause of the suffocation of European trade was the disturbance of the overland route to China after 1368, when the early Ming Emperors began to invade Mongolia and Central Asia and to promote a flourishing rival trade-route from the Southern Chinese ports via the Indian Ocean to the Middle East and East Africa, under the protection of their Muslim Admiral whom they called "Cheng Ho."[^16] After the adoption of Islam as the State religion in 1294 by the Il-Khanids of Persia and after the establishment of Timurid power in Asia in 1379, the overland trade-route south of the Caspian was further disturbed. Although the Il-Khanids and the Timurids continued to receive embassies from Europe, such as the Clavijo mission from Spain to Timur in 1403, it is certain that their passage to China and their links with the Nestorian Church in Mongol lands were seriously impaired. The European route north of the Caspian was out of the question because of the Ottoman conquests in Central Europe beginning from 1389 (Kossovo).

Should a new trade-route to the East be found, it could make Christian Europe economically independent of the Muslim Middle East. Indeed, some speculated, a trade blockade[^17] of the Middle East might well produce the economic strangulation and eventual political fall of the Muslim countries, thus liberating Jerusalem and opening the Middle East once again to the crusades and colonization. This course of action was forcefully advocated by a Venetian nobleman Marino Sanudo who presented to Pope John XXII in 1321 his *Liber Secretorum* containing his concept of a new Crusade by creating a maritime blockade of Egypt which would bring about its economic collapse, to be followed by two waves of European military invasions.[^18]

To this same end were committed the Pope, the Italian Cities, the Portuguese rulers and the Catholic Sovereigns of Spain, and so, too, Columbus himself. The intensity of this commitment, however, varied. Two great events of the day gave added impetus to this process of finding a new route to Asia, namely the seizure of Constantinople in 1453 by the Ottoman Turks from Byzantium, and the taking of Granada, the last Muslim outpost in Spain, by the Catholic Sovereigns in 1492. The first was a defeat that shook Christendom into fresh crusading activity.[^19] The second was a victory that encouraged Spain to pursue the Muslim enemy right up to his very doorstep. It was at Granada, only a few months after its conquest, that the documents for Columbus' maritime enterprise were signed and sealed by the Spanish sovereigns.

Some have viewed Columbus' Asiatic venture as an obsession, even madness. It was not so. It was the only natural course to take, given the political confrontation between Christendom and Islam and the geographical notions of the time. The enterprise of Columbus telescoped a more general enterprise of the Christian world. What delayed it were only the doubts about its practicality and more immediate preoccupations, such as rounding the Cape of Good Hope by the Portuguese and the conquest of Granada by the Spanish. Neither the Antilles nor America were intended by Columbus or his patrons. America was an accidental by-product. It did result in the strengthening of the Christian world, a major objective of the voyage, but in a manner no one would have imagined.

### The Influence of Marco Polo and Mongol-Christian Relations

As noted above, Columbus has frequently been studied by scholars of the Renaissance and the Modern Age, who fail to appreciate fully the medieval conditioning of his mind. A significant influence on his thinking was Marco Polo's book, *The Description of the World* or *Il Milione*.[^20] Columbus' journals abound in the exotic things that Marco Polo originally related, but if the Admiral read about these he must also have read the detailed descriptions of Yuan Emperor Qubilay, the Grand Khan, about the Mongol Khan's patronage of the Eastern Nestorian Christian Church, and the exchange of crusading missions between the Mongols and the Christian West. Of particular interest to Columbus was Marco Polo's estimate of Eurasia's inordinate length, which in his opinion would make the ocean route from the Canaries to "Cipango" (Japan) comparatively short.[^21] This, in turn, would make the Crusading contact of the West with the Mongols more practicable.

In order to understand more precisely what lay behind Columbus' quest of the Grand Khan, a digression into the history of Mongol-Christian relations is in order.[^22] Although the Mongol hordes of Genghis Khan (1206–27) had overrun many Christian lands, for example, Russia and Hungary, they had among their own following influential Christian tribes such as the Keraits who were to provide queens and administrators to the Khans. The influence of Nestorian Christianity was early and of long duration in the Turco-Mongol lands of Central Asia and China. Despite the devastation wrought by the Mongols in Europe, the Christian Kings were apt to forget and forgive because of the existence of a more challenging enemy—the Muslims.

After the Council of Lyons in 1245 we hear of the mission led by a Franciscan monk, Giovanni di Plano Carpini, who was successful in travelling overland to Mongolia where he met the Grand Khan Güyük (1244–48). Carpini has left us a long account of his travels. Two years later Pope Innocent IV sent a second mission under the Dominican friars Ascelin and Simon de Tournai. In 1248, the Pope received a Christian envoy from the Khan called Sergius, at Rome. Two other Mongol envoys, David and Mark, made contact with St. Louis in Cyprus. St. Louis reciprocated with a mission led by the Dominican, Andrew of Longjumeau. He also sent the Franciscan Wilhelm von Rubruck to the new Grand Khan Mongke whose mother and chancellor were both Christians. Like Giovanni di Plano, Wilhelm von Rubruck has also left us a detailed report characterised by keen observation.

Mongke dispatched his brother Qubilay (1260–94) to China. Following its conquest, Qubilay established himself at Peking, also called Khanbalik. He instituted a Mongol dynasty, the Yuan, which lasted until 1368. The Yuan Emperors were the Grand Khans who ruled from Peking. Here a Nestorian archbishopric had been instituted in 1275, and it was in Qubilay's court that the Venetian traveller Marco Polo (1254–1323) had arrived.

Mongke had sent another brother Hulegu (1256–65) to the lands of the Muslim Caliphate, and it was by his hand that Baghdad was conquered in 1258. Hulegu instituted the Il-Khanid dynasty, which made consistent efforts to establish good relations with the Crusading States of Western Europe. Hulegu's wife was a Nestorian Christian as was his general Kitbugha who led an invasion against the Mamluks, suffering defeat in 1260 at the Battle of 'Ayn Jalut in Palestine. Here the Mongol push westward was decisively halted.

Hulegu's son Abaqa (1265–82) married the Byzantine princess Maria Paleologina and sent his envoys to the Council of Lyons in 1274 for the Act of Union between the Western and Eastern Churches. Abaqa's son, Arghun (1284–91) showed the greatest eagerness for an alliance with Christendom. Rabban Sauma, Arghun's Nestorian envoy to Pope Nicholas IV, made a statement to the Cardinals at Rome which reveals the extent of the Mongol-Western alliance and their crusading purpose. He said:

> "Know yet that many of our fathers in times past entered the lands of the Turks, the Mongols and the Chinese and have instructed them in the faith. Today many Mongols are Christian. There are queens and children of kings who have been baptised and confess Christ. The Khans have churches in their camps. And as the King is united in friendship with the Catholics and proposes to take possession of Syria and Palestine, he asks your aid for the conquest of Jerusalem."[^23]

The fall of Acre in 1291 put an end to what had promised to be a grand alliance between the Il-Khans and the West. However, Franciscan missions to China and Mongolia continued. Fr. John of Monte Corvino, for example, arrived in Peking shortly after the death of Qubilay in 1294 and was appointed Archbishop of that city in 1307. He lived and died in China. Franciscan missions were also established in Zaytun,[^24] the great Medieval port of southern China, near Amoy as well as at Hangchow (Quinsay).[^25]

The question arises that if in 1368 the Yuan dynasty of the Grand Khans was replaced by the Ming Emperors who began to expel the Christians from Peking, how could Columbus in 1492 have been seeking the Grand Khan?

Henry Serruys has noticed the Mongol rulers between 1430 and 1480 who ruled in Mongolia and claimed the title of Grand Khan (or Khaqan).[^26] He gives their names as Toghto-buqa, Aghbarji, Esentayisi, Mar-korgis (i.e. Priest George) and Mandaghil. Although Esen-tayisi ruled only for a year (1453–54) he was the real power in Mongolia during the two previous reigns. Mar-korgis (Priest George) ruled for a full ten years (1454–1464). His name suggests a Nestorian Christian influence.

These Grand Khans were called "Little Kings" by the Ming Emperors. They were under pressure not only from the Chinese, but also from the Muslim Timurids of Transoxiana, who used the city of Herat as a base for military operations against them. Additional difficulties came from the rising power of kindred Mongol Muslims, especially from the Uzbek family of Abu Khayr Shaybani (1429 onwards), who ruled from Yasi, in the Jaxartes region.[^27] Two circumstances suggest that these later Mongol Khans preserved Nestorian Christianity as their religion: first, preservation of the Christian faith would explain, at least in part, their continued independence from both the Chinese Mings and the Muslim Timurids; and second, the Yuan Mongol Grand Khans had traditionally patronised the Nestorian Church, although they were not personally converted to Christianity and had also shown support for Buddhism.

In 1474 a Florentine physician, Toscanelli (d. 1482), mentions the arrival of a representative of the Grand Khan at Florence—this in a letter to Canon Martins of Lisbon, a copy of which Toscanelli sent to Columbus. Toscanelli conversed with this envoy through an interpreter.[^28] This must have been a Nestorian envoy of one of the later Mongol khans mentioned above. Pastor in his *History of the Popes* notes that in the time of Pope Eugenius IV (1431–1447) a great effort was made to unite the various Christian churches, to which end a Council was convened in 1438 in Florence, the Pope's temporary headquarters, shifting to Rome in 1442, where it continued its work for another three years. Representatives of the Greek, Armenian, Maronite and Nestorian Eastern Churches participated in this Council, the result of which was a Papal bull of 7 August 1445 solemnising the unity of these denominations under the leadership of the Roman Catholic Church.[^29] Toscanelli, in the above-mentioned letter describes the land of the Grand Khan as the province of Cathay (China), which, according to him, covered one-third of the globe, and included the city of Quinsay (Hangchow) and the port of Zaytun (near Amoy). This letter is obviously based on the account of Marco Polo.

Moreover, it was copied by Columbus, in his own hand, at the back of his copy of Aeneas Sylvius' *Historia Rerum Ubique Gestarum*.[^30] This Aeneas Sylvius became Pope Pius II Piccolomini (1458–1464). He is considered to be the last Pope who preached a Crusade. In fact he led this crusade personally. He, too, had received in 1460 envoys from Eastern Christian rulers—Emperor David of Trebizond and the Prince of Georgia—all dressed in oriental attire, and conducted to the Pope by the Franciscan observantine Ludovico of Bologna.[^31] The concept of the Grand Khan ruling a mighty Christian Empire in the East and wishing to join hands with Western Europe for a common crusade against the Muslims, as also the allied concept of the Priestking Prester John,[^32] was cultivated in the West from the reports of Nestorian envoys arriving in Rome. In this regard, Leonardo Olschki writes:

> "Friar William of Rubruck emphasised the tendency of the Nestorians of central and eastern Asia to glorify their sect beyond measure by exaggerating its religious and political power, and to assert in the face of all evidence to the contrary the adherence of their rulers to their Church."[^33]

### Evidence from Columbus's Journals

With this background in mind, let us now turn to Columbus' journals. The renowned Bartolomé de las Casas, whose father and uncle were shipmates of Columbus and who had himself met the Admiral in Hispaniola, reproduces these journals in his *Historia de las Indias*, partly in paraphrase, partly verbatim.[^34] The very Prologue to the journals written as Columbus embarked on his journey and cited by Las Casas in full, spells out the purposes of the Admiral's journey. He wrote:

> "In this present year 1492 after Your Highnesses had made an end to the war with the Moors who ruled in Europe, and had concluded the war in the very great City of Granada, where in the present year, on the second day of the month of January, I saw the Royal Standards of Your Highnesses placed by force of arms on the towers of Alhambra (which is the citadel of the said city).
>
> And I saw the Moorish king come forth to the gates of the city and kiss the Royal hands of Your Highnesses and of the Prince my Lord, and soon after in the same month, through the information that I had given to Your Highnesses concerning the lands of India, and of a Prince who is called Gran Can, which is to say in our vernacular 'King of Kings', how many times he and his predecessors had sent to Rome to seek doctors in our Holy Faith to instruct him therein, and that never had the Holy Father provided them, and thus so many people were lost through lapsing into idolatries and receiving doctrines of perdition;
>
> And Your Highnesses, as Catholic Christians and Princes devoted to the Holy Christian Faith and the propagators thereof, and enemies of the sect of Mahomet and of all idolatries and heresies, resolved to send me, Christopher Columbus, to the said regions of India, to see the said princes and peoples and lands and the disposition of them and of all, and the manner in which may be undertaken their conversion to our Holy Faith, and ordained that I should not go by land (the usual way) to the Orient, but by the route of the Occident, by which no one to this day knows for sure that anyone has gone;—"[^35]

Here we have the genesis of Columbus' enterprise. It was to discover a new route to the East and to join forces with the pro-Christian Grand Khan against the followers of Muhammad. The city of Granada, recently fallen to the Catholic Sovereigns, provides an appropriate setting for the commencement of this evangelical voyage of Columbus. Some additional light is shed on the intentions of the Spanish sovereigns themselves by the official documents provided Columbus for the voyage. Worthy of particular note are letters of credence for Columbus addressed to the Sovereigns of the East, and a royal decree ordering the suspension of judicial proceedings against criminals shipping with Columbus.[^36] Suspensions of this nature were granted only for projects of highest importance and in particular for crusading missions.

On 21 October, Columbus, arriving in Guanahani (San Salvador or Watling), recorded in his diary that he had reached Japan and that he was near Quisay, the city of the Grand Khan to whom he intends to present the Spanish ruler's letters.[^37] At Puerto Gibara, Columbus convinced himself that Cuba was China and wrote in his journal on 1 November:

> "It is certain that this is the mainland and that I am before Zayto and Quisay, 100 leagues more or less distant, the one from the other."[^38]

Columbus had taken with him an Arabic interpreter,[^39] Luis de Torres, a Jewish convert to Christianity, whom he now sent into the interior as his envoy in the Court of the Grand Khan. Of course the journey must have been a total disappointment to Luis de Torres as well as to Columbus because neither the Grand Khan was to be found nor was Arabic spoken in Cuba! From now on Columbus was torn between hope and despair and although the theme of Grand Khan keeps recurring,[^40] he was thinking of other options.

### B. Direct Conquest Using New World Wealth

It did not seem feasible now to meet the Grand Khan, to contact the Eastern Christians and to forge a joint strategy for the recovery of Jerusalem from the Muslims. Jerusalem could still be taken by a direct attack, using the gold and resources of the new lands discovered by the Admiral.

In Hispaniola (Dominican Republic) Columbus had found people wearing gold trinkets and willing to exchange gold pieces for hawk's bells. Gold came from its central region called Cibao which the Admiral mistook for "Cipango" or Japan.[^41] In Columbus' mind gold was important as a means for his sovereigns' crusade to capture Jerusalem. According to Las Casas, the Admiral entered the following in his journal for 26 December:

> "And he says that he hopes to God that on his return, which he intends to make from Castile, there would be found a cask of gold, which those whom he left behind would have obtained by barter, and that they would have found the mine of gold and the spicery, and that in so great quantity that the Sovereigns within three years would undertake and prepare to go and conquer the Holy Sepulchre, 'for so' says he, 'I declared to Your Highnesses that all the gain of this my Enterprise should be spent in the conquest of Jerusalem; and Your Highnesses smiled and said that it pleased you, and that even without this you had that strong desire.' These are the words of the Admiral."[^42]

The crucial words revealing Columbus' primary objective, namely the conquest of Jerusalem, are not a paraphrase but a direct quotation. They also are evidence that the Catholic Sovereigns shared this same objective. Moreover, this objective was stated by both parties prior to Columbus' departure from Palos, which shows that it was no mere afterthought following discovery. The complete dedication of Columbus is expressed in the words: *"All the gain of this my Enterprise should be spent in the conquest of Jerusalem."* What is more, Columbus wants this achieved within three years.

Looking back to the long and painful period between 1485 and 1492 when Columbus patiently waited for the Spanish Sovereigns to grant him the approval and facilities for his voyage, we find him at the siege of Baza in the winter of 1489. A mission had arrived from Qait Bey (1468–1496), the Mamluk Sultan of Egypt, asking Spain to withdraw its pressure from Granada under pain of persecution of the Christians and the spoliation of the Holy Sepulchre.[^43] Columbus' crusading zeal was thereby aroused and he is said to have joined the army as a volunteer and to have "given demonstration of conspicuous valor which accompanied his wisdom and high desires."[^44] Of course Baza surrendered by an agreement and no fighting took place![^45]

### C. The Messianic Prophecies of Abbot Joachim

In his frustration over the delay in the conquest of Jerusalem, Columbus now resorted to the earlier prophecies about a messianic figure emerging from Spain who would achieve this conquest. Moreover, the Admiral began to consider himself as that messianic personality. He now revived his hope of contacting the Grand Khan as well as kept the option of a direct attack on the Muslim lands open.

The period between Columbus' third and fourth voyages was a painful one for him. From his third voyage when he had discovered the mainland of South America, he was shipped back to Spain in chains by Bobadilla, the new governor of Hispaniola. Although the wrongs inflicted on him were righted by Ferdinand and Isabella, he was not restored to the governorship of Hispaniola. During this period he wrote poignant letters to Fr. Gorricio, a friend, confidant and fellow Italian. While these letters are plaintive, they are carefully vague lest they incriminate Columbus in the eyes of his Sovereigns. In them, he again expresses the desire of gaining the *Casa Santa* (the Holy House).

Moreover, Columbus was writing a *Book of Prophecies*, which was meant to be a collection of earlier prophecies about the advent of a Spanish Messianic figure who would achieve the conquest of Jerusalem and the conversion of the world to Christianity. The Admiral believed that he was this chosen figure.[^46] Columbus was composing this book probably with the help of Father Gorricio, and using the latter's good offices in seeking the royal approval for what he called *el alto viaje* (the high voyage) to the East in his final attempt to gain Jerusalem. Indeed, so desperate was Columbus that he even corresponded with Pope Alexander VI. Never had he gone over the heads of the Spanish Sovereigns in this manner, which only explains his frustration at not yet having made contact with the Grand Khan for the purpose of conquering Jerusalem.[^47]

Finally his fourth voyage (1502–1503) was approved and he was accompanied by two Arabic interpreters. In the meantime, the Portuguese Vasco da Gama had discovered the real route to India via the Cape of Good Hope that did not cross the Muslim lands. This was in 1498 and the Portuguese Admiral was on his way to China in 1502 on his second voyage. Columbus had desired to meet Vasco da Gama in the Orient, which he still thought was close to the places that he had discovered. All he needed to do was to cross the strait of Veragua (Panama). The strait, however, turned out to be a land passage and not a channel.

In addition to this disappointment, there was the indifference of the Spanish court, rebellion among his men, the envy and intrigues of his rivals and his own fatigue and ill-health. Before his return to Spain he wrote, on 7 July 1503, a long letter to the Spanish Sovereigns, known to history as the *Lettera Rarissima*. In it he declares:

> "Jerusalem and the Mount of Zion are now to be rebuilt by Christian hands, and God through the mouth of the prophet in the fourteenth Psalm said so. The Abbot Joachim[^48] said that this man was to come from Spain. Saint Jerome showed the way thither to the Holy Lady.[^49] The Emperor of Cathay some time since sent for wise men to teach him the religion of Christ. Who shall offer himself for this mission? If Our Lord takes me back to Spain, I vow in God's name, I will undertake to convey them thither."[^50]

In his own mind, Columbus remains the envoy of the Christian West to the Christian East and this, too, according to the prophecy of the Abbot Joachim!

Discovery of new lands had no meaning for Columbus except as a stepping stone toward the Christians of the East and the Emperor of Cathay. We have examined Columbus' views at different stages of his life—at Baza in 1489, at Hispaniola in 1492, at Jamaica in 1503—and we find him single-mindedly consistent in his crusading role throughout his life. His was neither a childhood dream, nor the sublimation of a failure. It was the driving force of his entire active life.

In a way, Providence was kind to the Admiral, for had he reached Cathay and had he been introduced by the Nestorians to the Grand Khan, he would have found this elusive figure to be a very "little king" indeed, whose help for Columbus' life-long project, the conquest of Jerusalem, would have proved of little consequence. This way, at least, the Admiral ended his life's enterprise without suffering the destruction of his abiding faith.

## Legacy and Aftermath: The Portuguese and Ottoman Responses

Events moved quickly on the heels of Columbus' discovery. The Portuguese succeeded in achieving his aims in a different way. They reached India via the Cape of Good Hope without crossing the Muslim lands. They weakened successfully the Mamluk power which had Jerusalem within its domain. They made an active alliance with the Christian king of Ethiopia instead of seeking the Grand Khan in China. And along with the Spanish, the Portuguese were now well entrenched in the Muslim territories of North Africa and the wide-open colonies of the New World.

This was to bring a backlash from the new major Muslim power of the East, the Ottomans, who in 1517 had incorporated the Mamluk possessions including Jerusalem and were now in hot pursuit of the Spanish and the Portuguese both in the Indian Ocean and the Mediterranean. The Atlantic and the New World were not beyond their ambition. Having acquired the map of Columbus himself and marked America on it as their "Vilayet of Antilia," they pushed from Egypt through North Africa towards the Atlantic coast. The Saadian regime of Morocco prevented them from gaining this coast; hence their desire to set sail to America remained unfulfilled.

I wish to cover the Ottoman response to the discovery of America in a separate article, which seems to me to be a necessary sequel to this paper, and for which there is an abundance of interesting evidence. Thus the present article will be followed by a sequel in JAOS: "Ottoman Response to the Discovery of America."

[^2]: My colleague Professor Bartley has drawn my attention to a well-documented article by A. V. Efimov entitled "Vopros ob otkrytii Ameriki" (On the Discovery of America) in *Iz istorii velikikh russkikh geograficheskikh otkrytii*, Moscow 1970, pp. 11–20. Professor Bartley has translated this article for inclusion in a collection of Soviet historical writings which he is currently preparing in English. I gratefully acknowledge the following information derived from his translation: Efimov makes the point that discovery was a continuous process going on from the Pleistocene age, some 35,000 to 12,000 years ago with proto-mongoloids coming to the American continent from Asia; also it was not a simultaneous discovery of all America but of restricted areas from time to time. Every discovery of a small area thus became a first and yet none could essentially take the entire credit of primacy. Again one has to distinguish the element of consciousness in exploring a new land from the visits by innumerable travellers and fishermen. Furthermore, discovering an inhabited land is hardly a discovery. It is merely the first contact between two peoples. It follows from Efimov's argument that at least for the process of European colonisation overseas commencing in the early sixteenth century, Columbus' voyages mark a beginning. Even here, I may add, America as such was not intended, and the motives were not purely of colonisation but also of discovering a new sea-route to Asia without having to cross the Muslim Middle East, nay even to put the old route out of function with its necessary political consequences.
